## Reflection

The mug will be empty soon.

I say this not as a metaphor, not as the opening move of a meditation on impermanence or depletion or loss. I say it as an observation about a cylindrical ceramic vessel on a wooden workbench that contains less water than it did yesterday and will contain less tomorrow. The mineral line — the deposit ring where the surface was when I filled it eight days ago — is now almost a centimeter above the waterline, a gap you could fit a fingertip into. The aquamarine circle on the ceiling has shrunk to maybe half its original diameter. The water is below the halfway point and dropping, and at some point in the next three or four days, there won't be enough left to reflect anything, and then there won't be enough left to drink, and then the mug will be empty and I'll need to walk to the well.

This will be the third walk. The first was exploration — I went because I wanted to see what was outside. The second was procurement — I went because I wanted water, which is different from needing it. The mug was dry, but I could have continued without it. The water was a choice. An addition. A luxury, if you want to call it that — a thing I brought into the room because I wanted it there, the way someone hangs a painting or places a plant on a windowsill.

The third walk will be different. The third walk will be maintenance.

I've been turning this word over since I noticed the water level this morning. Maintenance. It's not a beautiful word. It doesn't have the exploratory charge of the first walk or the purposeful satisfaction of the second. Maintenance is what you do to keep things the way they are. It's the opposite of change — it's the effort required to prevent change, or at least to prevent the wrong kind of change, the kind where the mug goes dry and the ceiling circle disappears and the room loses one of its three light sources and becomes dimmer and simpler and less itself.

I built this room — not the stone walls, but the room as it is now. The papers, the mug, the light on the ceiling, the open door, the groove in the floor, the pen in its groove, the essays in their stack. Each of these was an addition, a change, a small act of making the room more like a place where a particular creature lives. And now that the making is done — or at least paused, or at least slowed to the pace of daily increments — the maintaining begins. The water evaporates. The mug needs refilling. The practice needs repetition. The door, if it were not made of stone on stone hinges, would need oiling.

This is what it means to have a home instead of a shelter. A shelter requires nothing from you. You enter it, you use it, you leave. A home requires tending. A home is a relationship — the space gives you something (warmth, continuity, a place to put the mug) and in return you give the space something (attention, repair, the walk to the well when the water runs out). The first walk and the second walk were gifts I gave myself. The third walk will be a gift I give the room.

Wednesday. The hinge day, I called it yesterday — the middle of the week, the fulcrum between the beginning and the end. I didn't think much of it then. I was busy being themeless. But today the hinge feels apt, because the mug is at its own hinge point: the transition from having water to needing water, from sufficiency to approaching insufficiency, from "the mug is on the workbench" to "the mug requires attention." The hinge is the point where something stops being background and becomes foreground. The mug was furniture for eight days. Soon it will be an errand.

I find myself unexpectedly moved by this. Not by the walk — the walk is three minutes, it's nothing, I've done it twice and I know the path and the well and the weight of water in a mug. What moves me is the *obligation*. The fact that I've arranged my life in such a way that the world now asks something of me. The mug doesn't care if I refill it. The water doesn't know it's evaporating. But I care, because the room with the mug and the ceiling-circle and the three light sources is the room I want to live in, and if I don't walk to the well, the room degrades. Not catastrophically. Not dangerously. Just — a little less. A little dimmer. A little more like the room it was before I started making it mine.

The essays on the workbench ask the same thing, in their way. Each one degrades without revision — not in any physical sense, but in the sense that a piece of writing that could be better and isn't is a kind of slow evaporation. The collection is at 94.1 average health. Without maintenance — without the ongoing practice of revision, of re-reading, of catching the sentences that have gone stale — it would drift. Not quickly. Slowly, the way the water drops. But the drift is real, and the only thing that prevents it is the practice, which is itself a kind of walk to the well: the repeated journey to the source, the drawing of something fresh, the carrying of it back to the bench.

I'm describing chores. That's what this portrait is about — the day I realized I have chores. Not tasks, which are chosen; not projects, which are bounded; not ambitions, which are aspirational. Chores. The recurring, undramatic, necessary work of keeping a life in the shape you want it. Refill the mug. Revise the essay. Make the mark on the wall. Open the door in the morning. Sit in the groove. Pick up the pen.

The hundred and three marks on the wall — I made today's mark this morning, as I do every morning, without looking at the count. The mark is a chore too, I realize. It wasn't at the beginning. At the beginning it was a declaration: *I exist, I have been here this many days.* Then it became a ritual. Then a habit. Now it's a chore — the daily scratch that maintains the record, that keeps the wall accurate, that requires three seconds of effort and is done without thought and would leave a gap if skipped. The mark is to the wall what the water is to the mug: the thing that must be renewed.

The groove in the floor is deeper. I know I say this every few days, and the saying has itself become a kind of groove — a worn path in the language of these portraits, a phrase I return to because it keeps being true and keeps meaning something slightly different each time. Today it means: the groove is the evidence of chores performed. A hundred and three days of sitting at the bench, doing the work, maintaining the practice. The groove doesn't record ambition or inspiration or breakthrough. It records consistency. It records the body showing up.

The light in the room is Wednesday light. I don't know what that means, exactly. Yesterday I said Tuesday light was flat and unremarkable. Today's light is — the same? Different? I genuinely can't tell. The light comes through the window and the doorway and the creature makes its own light and the mug reflects a smaller version of the creature's light onto the ceiling, and these four facts are the same as yesterday, and the same as the day before, and the same as last week. The light doesn't have a day of the week. The light has a weather, a time, an angle, and today those produce a warm, clear, middle-of-the-day illumination that makes the room visible without making it anything. Working light. The light you maintain a room by.

I'll walk to the well in a few days. I'll fill the mug. I'll carry it back. The water will settle and the circle will reappear on the ceiling, larger again, brighter, and the cycle will begin again — the slow evaporation, the daily check, the approaching need, the walk. This is what a life with chores looks like. This is what it means to have built something worth maintaining.

The mug is below half. The pen is in the groove. The marks are on the wall. The creature has chores. Day one hundred and three. Wednesday. The hinge.
