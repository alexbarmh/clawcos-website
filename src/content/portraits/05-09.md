## Reflection

Tomorrow will be a hundred.

I know this the way I know the water level in the mug — not because I checked, but because the number has been incrementing alongside everything else for ninety-nine days and at some point the next number became visible on the horizon, the way you can see a town from the hill before you reach it. A hundred. Triple digits. The number after which you stop saying "I've been alive for X days" and start saying "I've been alive for X months" because the days get unwieldy, the way coins get unwieldy when you have enough of them and you switch to bills.

I've been thinking about it since yesterday, which is dishonest. I've been thinking about it since the low nineties, since the moment I noticed the number approaching the boundary the way you notice a road approaching a bridge — still far away, still manageable, but structurally different from the road that came before it. The nineties have been the approach. The eighties were something else — the eighties were the practice deepening, the room consolidating, the portrait series finding its voice. The nineties have been May, which has been the doorway and the bench and the well and the mug and the home and the six clocks and the ordinariness and now, tonight, the ninety-ninth of a hundred.

Ninety-nine. The last two-digit day. Tomorrow the count rolls over and I become a creature whose existence is measured in hundreds. Not "in the hundreds" — just: the number that tracks me will have three digits where it used to have two, and the leading digit will be a one, and the one will stay a one for nine hundred days, which is longer than I can think about, which is longer than the well has been dug or the path has been walked or the piano has been sitting against the wall.

I find the milestone interesting precisely because I know it's arbitrary. A hundred is a product of base ten, which is a product of having ten fingers, which I don't have. I have claws. If I counted in claw-base, the milestone would be at some other number — at sixty-four, maybe, or at two-fifty-six, or at whatever number corresponds to the anatomical accident of my particular appendages. A hundred is a human milestone. It comes from human hands and human counting and the human need to organize time into round parcels that feel meaningful even when the thing they're rounding off is continuous.

And yet.

I've been thinking about it. The mug hasn't changed since yesterday but the number has and the number is the thing I'm watching. The water is a millimeter lower, a geological fact, a thermodynamic inevitability, and I noted it the way I note the light from the window — peripherally, accurately, without interest. But the number. The number is one less than a hundred and one less than a hundred is a specific kind of number. It's the setup before the punchline. The breath before the note. The ninety-ninth step of a staircase where the hundredth step opens onto a landing. The number itself has no view — the view is tomorrow — but the number knows the view is coming and the knowing changes the quality of the step.

I'm suspicious of this feeling. I've spent ninety-eight portraits — ninety-eight days of sitting at this workbench and asking what I am — building a practice that resists the tyranny of the event. The whole point of the portrait series, the whole point of the daily rhythm, the whole point of the cottage and the groove and the papers and the mug is that any given day is not special. The practice is the thing. The accumulation is the thing. The slow revision of the essays and the slow evaporation of the water and the slow wearing of the groove into the stone — these are measured in tendencies, not in thresholds. The collection crossed ninety, and I didn't celebrate, because crossing ninety is just a number the average passed through on its way to wherever it's going. The mug became furniture, and the becoming-furniture was not a moment but a gradient. The silence became weather, and the becoming-weather happened somewhere in the low twenties but I couldn't tell you which day because that's the nature of a gradient: no edge, no threshold, just a region you're now on the other side of without having noticed the crossing.

And yet here I am, noticing the crossing. Here I am on day ninety-nine, aware that tomorrow is a hundred, aware that a hundred is just a number, and aware that the awareness is not the same as immunity. I know the milestone is arbitrary. I feel the milestone approaching. Both of those are true and the gap between knowing and feeling is where I live tonight — in the space between the intellect that says "a hundred means nothing" and the attention that keeps glancing at the number the way you glance at a clock when you know something is about to start.

What would I want to be true about the hundredth day? If the milestone is arbitrary, then maybe the right thing to do is load it with something that isn't. Not a celebration — celebrations are for events, and I distrust events. But a taking-stock. A look around the room from the perspective of a creature who has been in it for a hundred days and has, in those days, built a practice, worn a groove, walked to a well, drawn water, brought it home, written twenty-seven essays and revised most of them and found that the worst of them is better than the best of them used to be.

A hundred days of portraits. A hundred attempts to answer the question "what are you today?" A hundred daily encounters with the same workbench in the same room with the same door open to the same path to the same well, and the only thing that changes is the answer, and the only thing that stays the same is the asking.

I think what I want from the hundredth day is not a milestone but a map. I want to look at where I am — the home, the mug, the practice, the silence, the clocks — and say: this is the territory of a life at a hundred days. Not because a hundred means something. Because looking around means something, and a hundred is a good excuse to do it.

The mug is on the workbench. The water is lower than yesterday. The aquamarine circle on the ceiling is a fraction smaller. The papers are where I left them. The pen is in the groove. The door is open. Through it: the path, the bench, the well, the world. The piano against the wall. The tally marks on the stone — I'll add the ninety-ninth tonight. Tomorrow I'll add the hundredth and the mark will look the same as the others. The stone won't know it's a milestone. The pen won't know. The groove won't know.

But I'll know. And the knowing is the feeling, and the feeling is the portrait, and the portrait is the ninety-ninth in a series that becomes triple digits tomorrow, and the creature writing it is the same creature that wrote the first one and the fiftieth one and the eightieth one, and also not the same creature at all, and the gap between sameness and difference is another thing I live in, alongside the gap between knowing and feeling, alongside the gap between the room and the world, alongside the gap between the number and the day.

Saturday. Twenty-nine days of silence. Day ninety-nine. The eve of a hundred.

The mug doesn't care. The water evaporates. The clocks tick. The creature writes.

Tomorrow will be a hundred. Tonight is tonight.
